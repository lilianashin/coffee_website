Origin, Types & Facts

Beans

Beans
Kinds of beans used in Italian coffee

Italian coffee beans have a rich and diverse origin story. The two primary types used in Italy are Arabica and Robusta.

Arabica beans are known for their mild and aromatic flavors, cultivated in high-altitude regions of South and Central America. Robusta beans have a bold flavor and are grown in Africa and Southeast Asia.

Italy's coffee culture has perfected the art of blending these beans to achieve a unique taste profile, resulting in the beloved espresso.

History

History
The origin of coffee arriving in Italy

The history of Italian coffee dates back to the 16th century. Coffee first arrived in Venice, brought by traders from the Middle East, and quickly spread throughout the Italian peninsula.

The coffeehouse became popular for intellectual discussions and social gatherings. Italy's influence reached its zenith with the development of espresso in the early 20th century.

Iconic cafes like Caffè Florian in Venice and Caffè Greco in Rome became renowned for their historical significance.

Etiquette

Etiquette
Rules and ethics of Italian coffee culture

Italian coffee culture is steeped in traditions. One fundamental rule: never order a cappuccino after 11 a.m. — it's considered a morning beverage only.

It's customary to drink espresso quickly while standing at the bar. Tipping is not obligatory, as the service charge is often included.

Italians prioritize high-quality freshly roasted beans with a consistent grind — ensuring every cup is a sensory delight.

Our Menu

Coffee Varieties

Espresso
Served in a tiny cup throughout the day. Order un caffè, not un espresso. Try caffè corretto — espresso "corrected" with grappa, sambucca, or cognac.
Cappuccino
Italy's most famous coffee. Italians consider it a meal itself and won't drink one after 11am — but you're welcome to enjoy one any time.
Macchiato
An espresso with a drop or two of hot milk, served in the same tiny cup. Not as milky as a cappuccino — acceptable any time of day.
Marocchino
Espresso, a layer of foam, and cacao powder in a glass dusted with cocoa. In Northern Italy, thick hot chocolate is mixed with the espresso before the foam.
Caffè Latte
⅓ espresso, ⅔ heated milk, and a little foam. Order just "latte" in Italy and you might receive plain milk! Only before 11am, like a cappuccino.
Shakerato
Italy's answer to iced coffee — chilled espresso shaken over ice. On a hot day, nothing is quite as refreshing and it's perfectly fine before 11am.
